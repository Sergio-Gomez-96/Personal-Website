Wing Design Calculations

I started by calculating the estimated weight of the drone based on historical data from general aircraft. The ratio of the aircraft weight when empty (\(W_{e}\)) to take-off weight (\(W_{TO}\)) is recommended to be about 0.78 from an article we referenced for the design of the drone. \[W_{e}/W_{TO} = 0.78\] The required payload weight is 10 lbs therefore: \[W_{e} = W_{TO}-10\] \[(W_{TO}-10)/W_{TO} = 0.78\] \[W_{TO} \approx 45.21 lbs\] With this approximate weight we were able to calculate the coefficient of lift using the equation: \[L = \frac{1}{2}\rho V^{2}SC_{L}\] Before we could calculate the coefficient of lift we needed to find the surface area of the wing (S). We were able to do this because we know that \(S = \frac{b^{2}}{AR}\) and from the same article it was noted that an AR (aspect ratio) of at least 6 was desirable for our application. And based on market data of VTOL drones with similar weight we found that a wing span (b) of 9.85 ft was expected for our design. When we plug in these values we get a wing area (S) of 14.2 \(ft^{2}\). The other variables are as follows: \[L=W_{TO}=54lbs\] \[\rho=0.046lbs/ft^{3}\] \[V=73.33ft/s^{2}\] We are able to reorganize the lift equation to solve for the coefficient of lift: \[C_{L}=\frac{2W_{TO}}{\rho V^{2}S}\] Our coefficient of lift is 0.827 and we use this value to select an airfoil.

Airfoil Selection

After searching for tools to compare airfoils to one another I found a Matlab application that was able to do this. I experimented with many types of airfoils like NACA 4 symmetric, NACA 4 cambered, NACA 5 symmentric, etc. but the airfoil type that achieved the necessary coefficient of lift for the drone was the NASA supercritical airfoil.

From here I looked for airfoils that were capable of achieving the coefficient of lift on airfoiltools.com and found 3 airfoils that were able to achieve the coeffient of lift that we needed. Our options were the NASA SC-0712, NASA SC-0714, and NASA SC-1010. I ultimately chose the NASA SC-0714 since it achieved the lift coefficient needed and the lift coefficient could increase to 1.60 at an angle of 11.5 degrees which I believed would be beneficial later.

Motor, ESC, and Propeller Calculation

Now that we had an estimated weight I was able to calculate the thrust needed to lift the drone. An article I read noted that the thrust to weight ratio should be 1.2. \[T/W_{TO}=1.2\] \[T=1.2\cdot 45.21lbs\] \[T\approx 54.25lbs\] We decided to use a quadmotor layout since it was the easiest configuration for us and it provides a stable flight without additional input correction from the flight controller. Thus, we divided the total thrust by 4 since this is the amount of thrust needed from each motor and propeller configuration. \[T_{m}=T/4\] \[T_{m}\approx 13.56lbs= 6157g\]

Finding a motor and propeller combination was the most difficult of this process and after many hours researching I found a motor and propeller combination from KDE Direct that was able to achieve the thrust we needed. The motors are the KDE5215XF-435 and the propellers and electronic speed controller (ESC) combination were recommended with these motors. The propellers that achieve our needed thrust were the 15.5" x 5.3 propellers in a triple blade layout. This combination with a 6s battery could produce a maximum thrust of 6540 g surpassing the 6157 g of thrust we needed.

Battery Selection Calculations

I used the table above to create this graph that compares the thrust output to the current input. Since this is experimental data it is the ideal method of calculating the power needs of the battery.

From this graph we were able to plot a polynomial trend line:

\[C=7\cdot 10^{-7}x^{2}+0.0047x\]

Where C is the input current in amps and x is the thrust output in grams. We know the thrust required of the drone in its different flight patterns by using a free body diagram and we are able to use this thrust to calculate the required current input to produce that thrust. We calculated power needs for the needed flight patterns.

Climb - When all four motors are producing thrust and the drone lifts in the air

Current (A) 55.5
Power (W) 1445.8
Time (hrs) 0.0083 (30 seconds)
Energy (A hrs) 0.46
Total Energy for 4 motors (A hrs) 1.85

In this flight mode the thrust output is the highest of any of the modes and this is because the thrust force must be greater than the weight of the drone. While in this mode the drone must climb to 400 ft at 4 m/s or about 13 ft/s. It would take approximately 30 seconds to reach this altitude. The total energy used for this flight mode is 1.85 A hrs since all 4 motors are being utilized to climb.

Forward flight - Only the front two motors pivot from vertical climb orientation to forward flight orientation

Current (A) 5.77
Power (W) 150.502
Time (hrs) 1
Energy (A hrs) 5.77
Total Energy for 2 motors (A hrs) 11.55

The drone spends most of its time in this flight mode to deliver packages and return to base. It is also the most efficient flight mode since the wings produce the lift the drone needs to maintain its altitude. We know that the forward thrust must be equal to the drag force since the drone is not accelerating and our forward flight velocity must be 73.34 ft/s based on our design requirements. Using this information I knew we needed the fron motors to produce 1061 grams of thrust which I input into the trendline to get the current needed.

Landing - This flight mode slowly decreases the altitude of the drone

Current (A) 29.29
Power (W) 763.36
Time (hrs) 0.025 (1.5 minutes)
Energy (A hrs) 0.73225
Total Energy for 4 motors (A hrs) 2.93

Based on some research papers I read it was recommended that drones should descend at about 13 ft/s however, I felt that descending it at a slower rate of 4.5 ft/s would be safer due to the the weight of the drone. At this rate the total time needed for descent would be 1 and a half minutes.

Hovering - In hover mode the drone stays in a stationary position above the ground

Current (A) 42.54
Power (W) 1108.81
Time (hrs) 0.2 (12 minutes)
Energy (A hrs) 8.51
Total Energy for 4 motors (A hrs) 34.04

Hover mode is the most energy consuming mode but is necessary when delivery a package. While in this mode a winch system lowers the payload package until safe to release the payload at which  point it retracts the winch and continues with the flight. The time needed for the winch to lower the payload and retract is estimated to take 5 minutes however, I added 7 more minutes of hover time in case of difficult conditions.

Based on this information we were able to select an appropriate battery to meet our design requirements.

20% Extra Capacity: 10 A hrs
Battery Size: 60 A hrs

Aerodynamics

We used an open source software called OpenVSP developed by NASA to help design the characteristics of the drone. Since we have calculated the necessary parameters, we are able to input them into OpenVSP and tweak the wing characteristics to design the drone.

The characteristics I set on the application were the wing span (b) at 9.84 ft, taper ratio of 0.3, 8 inch chord at the base of the wing, and an average chord length of 5.2 inches.

I added wing tips as it helps the stability of flight as well as increases the efficiency while in forward flight mode. I researched how the position of the wings affects the drones flight and decided to position the wings at the top of the drone. In this position the wings produce a very stable flight.

Final CAD Design

Once the drone was designed in OpenVSP I was able to use the dimensions and create a CAD representation in Solidworks. I mainly used the loft tool for the complex shaped wings and fuselage. In addition to this tool I use the shell, extrude, sweep, and dome tools to create the CAD model
