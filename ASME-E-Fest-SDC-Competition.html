ASME E-Fest SDC Competition

The American Society of Mechanical Engineers (ASME) hosted a virtual student design competition (SDC) that challenges student teams. This year’s competition challenged me in new ways including working with electrical circuits, understanding how to harness renewable energies, and mechatronics/robotics.

Project Background

This year’s competition required teams to design and build a vehicle capable of collecting solar and/or wind energy to extend the duration of operation while of a remotely controlled vehicle that navigates a course and transports weights to earn points. The only power source allotted on the vehicle is a single AAA battery.

Roles and Responsibilities

I had multiple roles and responsibilities on this project. The following are some of the highlights of the things I did in those roles and how I was able to accomplish the tasks.

I had the following responsibilities for this project:
- Energy Storage
- Solar Power
- Prototyping
- Testing

Energy Storage

The main goal of the project was our ability to store energy produced by the solar array. I came up with the solution of using supercapacitors as our main source of energy. I calculated the theoretical time it would take for the supercapacitors to charge and how long they would be able to power the motors. With the theoretical calculations I was able to determine the ideal amount of capacitors to use, the time needed to charge, and wiring configuration.

After additional research on safe ways to charge supercapacitors I learned that overcharging supercapacitors could result in the capacitors exploding. As a result I bought a protection circuit and soldered an indicator LED to it so that it lit up when the supercapacitors were fully charged.

Equations Used

The following are some of the equations I used to calculate the theoretical time it would take to charge a supercapacitor bank as well as the voltage of the bank when the motors are being used at a constant voltage.

Capacitors in Parallel

\[C_{T}=C_{1}+C_{2}+C_{3}+C_{n}\]

Capacitors in Series

\[C_{T}=(\frac{1}{C_{1}}+\frac{1}{C_{2}}+\frac{1}{C_{3}}+\frac{1}{C_{n}})^{-1}\]

Discharge Equation

\[V_{C}=V_e^{\frac{-t}{RC}}\]

Charging Equation

\[I=I_{S}e^{\frac{-t}{RC}}\]

Solar Power

It was my responsibility to choose the best configuration of solar cells based on experimental current and voltage data we tested. After experimenting with different configurations, I found that 6 series and 3 parallel was the best option. This configuration outputs almost 5 V at 2 Amps.

Prototyping

The main parts I focused on were the supercapacitor housing and the solar array panel. I used a digital caliper to get the dimensions of the supercapacitors and replicated one in Solidworks. I modeled the following part around it so that it would hold two supercapacitors. A teammate was in charge of manufacturing and used their home 3D printer to fabricate the part.

For the solar panel, we originally had a layout of 9 solar cells however I needed more power. This is why I added 9 more perpendicular to the perimeter. I created some L brackets to achieve the perpendicular shape and fastened it to the existing panel with durable double sided tape. 

Testing

We tested the power input that the solar panel was able to absorb. We also timed how long it took for the capacitors to be fully charged since there was an overall time limit of 15 minutes for the competition. The amount of time we would need to charge the capacitors to full was 20 minutes which is why we adjusted our strategy to charge for 6 minutes and begin the next phase after that. 

Lessons Learned

This project was the most experience I’ve had working with electronics. It is also the first project that I put in more work prototyping, testing, and fabricating than I did designing parts and systems in Solidworks..

One of the biggest lessons I learned was that I wasn't as good at circuits as I thought I was. I passed my circuits class with a B and understood the material well in class however, when it came to working on real circuits I found it more difficult to apply my knowledge and understanding to the real life applications. It required me thinking critically about the problems I needed to solve before I could apply the academic concepts.

I also learned a great deal about certain electronics from this project such as solar cells, supercapacitors, battery charging, and buck-boost converters. In my experience with solar cells I found out how fragile and sensitive solar cells are. These were difficult to solder and mount to the solar array due to how thin, flexible, and brittle the solar cells were. Supercapacitors were an interesting part for me because I was able to learn about a new type of energy storage that had many pros like much faster charging rates! But it severely lacks the energy storage capacity compared to batteries.

From this project I was able to work in a fast paced team environment that required me to be flexible and wear multiple hats.
