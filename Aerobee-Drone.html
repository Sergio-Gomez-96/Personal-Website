Project Information

Overview

Currently in the industry, the plan for delivery drones is to make one trip per package due to the weight limitations. What we’ll be doing differently is delivering multiple packages within a single trip to maximize the time of flight of the drone. We will also try to match the DHL’s Parcelcopter delivery time which consisted within 8 minutes of take-off. This means that we will have to optimize the characteristics to have our drone travel around 43 miles per hour.

Design Requirements

Design stage requirements

The primary objective of the design was to allow for both agile and dynamic movement, while ensuring stability in taking off, landing, hovering and cruising under considerable payload. As a result we were able to condense the design requirement to 6 main objectives. We based our design requirements around similar drones currently in the market.

PAYLOAD

The aircraft must have the capability of transporting 10 lbs of package payload.

WEATHER

The aircraft must be able to withstand windy conditions of the Northern California area while assuring necessary stable hovering and cruising conditions for carrying aforementioned payload.

HYBRID DRONE

The front propeller arms must rotate 90 degrees from horizontal to vertical in order to allow the UAV to convert the VTOL between flight modes.

RANGE

The aircraft must be able to travel a 10 mile radius from takeoff.

FLIGHT TIME

The aircraft must be able to fly for an hour and have 20% battery remaining for emergency.

AUTONOMY

The aircraft must be able to perform autonomous flight as well as a manual override option in extreme circumstances.

Aerodynamics

Prior to working on this project, none of our teammembers had experience with designing or even working on a flying vehicle that uses wings to generage lift. To create a design of the wings and an select an appropriate airfoil it is important to know which factors are required. The first place to look is in the lift equation, defined below:Once I understood the equation it was clear that the main aspects that affect the lift generated from the wings were the lift coefficient, aircraft speed, air properties, and wing area. The velocity of the vehicle and air density are defined by design criteria and googling the air density at 400 ft altitude. We know the lift force must be equal to the weight at take-off which we have estimated based on market condition. The last thing we need to define before we can clearly define the coefficient of lift is the wing area.

We defined our wingspan to be 2 meters based on market research of drones with the capability of lifting heavy objects such as cameras. We decided to add a taper ratio of 0.3 to reduce the amount of weight at the tips of the wings and reduce the induced drag on the wings. The root chord length is 0.2032 m and the tip chord length was defined to be 0.06096 m with an average chord of 0.132 m. Based on a research article referenced, it was noted that a high aspect ratio was desirable for efficient vehicles and a higher AR than 6 should be a goal when designing the wings. Based on our calculations our AR was defined to be 7.57 from an open source software called OpenVSP.

As our UAV has VTOL attribute, a payload of 10 lbs, and a proposed cruise speed of 45 mph, we were looking to achieve both mobility and speed while guarantee stabilization in take-off, landing and cruising. Accordingly, we expected to minimize yaw, especially during vertical take-off and landing. 

To achieve our goals, a team member suggested an approach for designing the tail. In such, the H-tail notion is adapted with swept-back horizontal stabilizer, combining with proposed design of the wing in order to reduce yaw while vertical take-off and landing and reduce drag while cruising. Performing simulation using OpenVsp, we achieved 0.04839 as the drag coefficient for the tail, which meets our expectation of 0.05 drag coefficient for overall wing design.

The coefficient of drag value is used to quantify the drag or resistance of an object in a fluid environment, such as air or water. The drag coefficient for an airplane wing should be 0.05. We made it our goal to achieve a number that is equal to or lower than 0.05. To achieve this number we had to perform modifications on certain wing characteristics.

Our team worked on researching the best airfoils used for plane wings, and implemented them into the design for our drone wing. We then agreed to use an airfoil called the NASA SC-0714 which provided us with the highest coefficient of lift we could find. Our goal was to have a coefficient of lift of 1.6. We also needed to determine some characteristics that the wing needed in order to meet some of the calculations. Those characteristics included: wingspan, taper ratio, tip length, root length, average chord and Aspect Ratio.

In order to come up with these characteristics and have some calculations to back up our choices, we used a program called OpenVsp. The program allowed us to import the airfoil we chose and analyze the wing. With OpenVsp we hoped to gain a reasonable coefficient of drag, as well as other values that would allow us to determine whether our wing would work or not. 

Material Selection

In the quest of determining the right material for the UAV structure, we set a standard of high specific strength and low density in order to satisfy the need for a material that is both lightweight and robust for our drone. We also wanted a material that could be 3D printed since it would result in us being able to print the wings which allowed us to design more complex wings.

After some short searching online we found a solution in eSUN's new lightweight PLA specifically made for RC planes. It is lighter than traditional printer filaments since it has a greater foaming rate. The foaming rate also allows the layers to adhere to each other better and provide a much smoother finish. Structure Analysis

The function of the fuselage is to house the components necessary to the functionality of the drone as well as to provide the structure of the aerial vehicle. The dimensions of the fuselage must be chosen to minimize the volume used as this will cut down on the weights of the overall drone. For the main cabin we optimized to have the package located in this area and we allotted 10 mm of space on all sides around the package compartment to provide enough space for the package to fit comfortably. A larger cabin would result in a higher drag which is why we opted to leave the apartment as small as possible. The fuselage tapers towards the wing to decrease the amount of space needed in total and this space allows for the electrical components to be housed away from all the other components. Since the electronics need to be accessible when the drone is fully done, we allotted to have the tail open and this would allow for any tinkering of the electronic devices or even replacement of these electronic devices.

For the main fuselage housing, an inner grid pattern was designed so that the skin of the fuselage could remain structurally sound. Overall the skin of the whole drone was designed to be approximately 2 mm thick. The wings utilized a rib design that allowed for 8 mm thick carbon fiber tubes to run through them and secure to the main frame.

The tail of the drone also uses the same 8 mm carbon fiber tube for reinforcement. The motor mounts utilize 25 mm carbon fiber tubes that attach directly to the frame. The landing gear of the drone uses 20 mm carbon fiber tubes.

Pixhawk 4® is an advanced autopilot designed and made in collaboration with Holybro® and the PX4 team. It is optimized to run PX4 v1.7 and later, and is suitable for academic and commercial developers.

The Pixhawk allows for the design of an autonomous drone without having the painstaking need to code all the parameters. Many manufacturers have recognized the Pixhawk and have created user guides for their ESCs and motors to connect, which is what the manufacturer for our ESCs did.

We bought our Pixhawk 4 unit from Holybro in a bundle that included a power management board and an external GPS unit. Since the flight controller was bought as a bundle, it was straight forward on how to connect the components to one another based on the included wiring diagram.

We decided to go with KDE Direct motors since we found a publication about the Wingcopter drone utilizing motors from this manufacturer. I calculated the thrust required for our drone by dividing the total take-off weight by 4 since there would be 4 motors. After, I multiplied this value by 1.2 since this is generally a safe thrust to weight ratio based on research articles we cited.

Using this thrust value we were able to choose the right voltage battery and select the propellers based on the graph provided from KDE Direct. In addition to the propellers, KDE also provided ESCs that work in conjuction with the ​aforementioned motor and propeller combination.

Propellers: 12.5 inches in triple blade configuration
ESCs: KDE Direct UAS 35A+ Multi-Rotor Series

Since we had the power consumption of the motors from KDE based on the thrust produced, I was able to use that data to create theoretical power consumption of the battery based on different flight modes. Those flight modes are altitude climb, hover, forward flight, and landing. I found that climb and hover consumed the most amount of power from the battery. Thankfully the drone is able to climb to its intended altitude quickly meaning it won't drain the battery too long and the drone does not need to hover for long either.

Once I was able to compile the power consumption needs I added 25% of the power needed since research articles found that usage of 75% of a drones battery was ideal for a safe and reliable flight.

Based on the calculations, I found that a 6s 10,000 mAh battery would be ideal for our power needs while weighing about 5 pounds for our prototype.

Lessons Learned

Coming into my capstone project I had a decent amount of experience working on projects whether they were conceptural or hands on. As a result I was confident in my abilities to run this project as the lead engineer. Although this project was my idea, it was still my most challenging project since this is the first time I was experiencing the process of mechanical design and merging different diciplines such as electrical and aerospace engineering.

As a result, I am confident that I have learned a ton from this project. First, I finally experienced what it is like to set design requirements based on market data and compare current designs in the market. Next, I experienced aerodynamic engineering for the first time and was able to understand the fundamentals of how an aerial vehical is able to generate lift from wings. I was able to learn about lift equations, especially how wing span and wing area affect lift and efficiency of flight. I also learned how to select an airfoil based on the coefficient of lift needed. I also researched and was able to choose the ideal type of tail and the details of the tail based on the coefficient of the moment from multiple axis.

Finally, I expanded my understanding of brushless motors and propellers and how they generate thrust. I had a good understanding before this project however, I was able to use that experience and choose some motor, propeller, and ESC combinations that suited our design. I was also able to understand how to choose a suitable battery based on power need calculations for altitude, hover, forward flight, and descent.
